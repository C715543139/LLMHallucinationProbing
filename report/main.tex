\begin{abstract}
Hallucination detection is often treated as an output-level problem, but a language model may already encode evidence about factual correctness in its internal activations. This report studies binary factuality detection on a True-False statement dataset using Qwen2-1.5B. We compare a perplexity baseline with SAPLMA-style hidden-state probes, analyze which layers and token positions expose the strongest signal, and evaluate attention-guided feature extensions. Perplexity provides weak but non-random evidence, reaching 52.93\% accuracy and 0.6784 AUROC on the test set. Hidden-state probing is much stronger: a final-layer logistic probe reaches 74.96\% accuracy and 0.8265 AUROC, while an MLP probe reaches 77.71\% accuracy and 0.8770 AUROC. Layer analysis further shows that factuality signals concentrate in middle-to-late layers; the validation-selected layer 17 reaches 80.03\% accuracy and 0.8876 AUROC. Full Phase 4 ablation shows that debiased attention-score-only features (A2) reach 81.93\% accuracy, 81.93\% macro-F1, and 0.9010 AUROC, making A2 the strongest attention-guided variant in the A0-A9 comparison. These results support the view that internal states provide more reliable hallucination signals than output likelihood alone, and that attention scores themselves provide strong factuality evidence.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) can produce fluent answers that are factually wrong. This behavior, commonly called hallucination, is a central obstacle to reliable use of LLMs in knowledge-intensive settings such as education, scientific assistance, legal support, and decision making \citep{ji2023survey,li2023halueval}. A practical detector should identify when a generated or evaluated statement is likely to be false, but the signal used for detection matters. Output likelihood and confidence are easy to obtain, yet they primarily measure how natural a sequence is under the model, not whether the sequence is true.

This project follows a different hypothesis: factuality may be more directly visible inside the model than in the final likelihood assigned to a sentence. Prior work argues that LLM activations can contain linearly or non-linearly readable truthfulness information \citep{azaria2023internal,burns2022discovering}. If this is true, hallucination detection can be framed as a probing problem: freeze the LLM, extract internal features from a statement, and train a lightweight detector over those features.

We evaluate this hypothesis on a True-False statement dataset using Qwen2-1.5B \citep{yang2024qwen2}. The task is binary: given a statement, predict whether it is true or false. The project is organized around three questions. First, how far can a simple perplexity (PPL) baseline go? Second, do hidden states expose stronger factuality signals, and which layers or token positions are most useful? Third, can attention-derived features provide complementary evidence beyond hidden states?

Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item We implement and compare PPL and SAPLMA-style hidden-state probes under a fixed train/validation/test split.
  \item We analyze layer depth and token pooling, showing that middle-to-late layers and last-token representations are substantially more informative.
  \item We design attention-guided extensions based on statement anchors, attention scores, head selection, attention outputs, feature fusion, and gated routing.
  \item We provide full A0-A9 ablations, case-level error analysis, and visualizations showing which attention-derived signals are effective and which complex fusion paths have limited full-data gains.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Hallucination detection.}
Hallucination in natural language generation refers to generated content that is fluent but unsupported, inconsistent, or factually incorrect \citep{ji2023survey}. Benchmarks such as HaluEval evaluate hallucination behavior across tasks and model outputs \citep{li2023halueval}. Many applied systems address hallucination using retrieval, external fact checking, or post-hoc verification. These approaches are valuable, but they rely on external evidence sources and do not directly answer whether the model's own computation contains a factuality signal.

\paragraph{Likelihood and uncertainty signals.}
A natural internal baseline is likelihood. If a model assigns lower loss to true statements than to false statements, sequence probability or PPL can be used as a detector. However, likelihood is affected by frequency, style, surface fluency, and length. A false but familiar-sounding statement may have low PPL, while a true but rare statement may have high PPL. For this reason, likelihood is best treated as a baseline factuality signal.

\paragraph{Probing internal representations.}
The internal-state view is motivated by work showing that truthfulness and latent knowledge can be extracted from model activations \citep{azaria2023internal,burns2022discovering}. Instead of asking whether the model's next-token probabilities directly reveal truth, probing asks whether hidden states contain a representation from which truth can be predicted. This report adopts that framing through SAPLMA-style classifiers over frozen Qwen2-1.5B activations.

\paragraph{Attention as diagnostic signal.}
Transformer attention \citep{vaswani2017attention} can expose structured interactions between tokens. Our advanced experiments use attention as a feature family: attention mass from the last token to statement anchors, validation-selected heads, and attention-output activations are tested as signals for factuality detection.

\section{Task and Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Task Definition}

Each example is a declarative statement \(x\) with a binary label \(y \in \{0,1\}\), where \(1\) denotes a true statement and \(0\) denotes a false statement. A detector receives the statement and outputs both a binary prediction and a continuous score. The continuous score is used for AUROC, while the binary prediction is used for accuracy and macro-F1.

This setting uses statement-level probing: we directly test whether the model's internal computation over a given statement separates factual from non-factual inputs.

\subsection{Dataset}

The processed dataset contains 6,309 effective examples from seven CSV sources: animals, cities, companies, chemical elements, scientific facts, inventions, and an additional generated subset. The first six domains match the course task description; the generated subset is included in the current project configuration. All main results are reported on the fixed split that includes the generated subset, and this extension preserves the six-domain core of the course dataset. The split is fixed at 8:1:1 and stratified by domain and label.

\subsection{Model and Feature Extraction}

All experiments use Qwen2-1.5B as the frozen base model. We extract hidden states and attention tensors through the Hugging Face Transformers stack \citep{wolf2020transformers}. The LLM is never fine-tuned; only lightweight downstream classifiers are trained. For Phase 4, the stable execution path is eager attention with bfloat16. Earlier float16 runs produced NaNs in representative hidden and attention slices, so the reported advanced results use the bfloat16 rerun.

The hidden-state experiments use layer indices over the 28 Transformer blocks, excluding the embedding output from the layer numbering. Token pooling variants include the first token, mean pooling over valid tokens, and the final valid token. The attention experiments define statement anchors over subject, relation, tail, and last-token positions, then compute attention-score features and attention-output features from selected layers and heads.

\subsection{Metrics}

We report accuracy, macro-F1, and AUROC. Accuracy measures thresholded correctness, macro-F1 accounts for class balance, and AUROC measures ranking quality under continuous detector scores. AUROC is especially important because a practical detector may adjust its decision threshold for different risk levels.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Perplexity Baseline}

The PPL baseline uses the language modeling loss of the full statement. Given a tokenized statement, we compute the average causal language modeling loss \(\mathcal{L}(x)\) and define:

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{PPL}(x) = \exp(\mathcal{L}(x)).
\end{equation}

Lower PPL means that the model finds the statement more likely. We use validation data to select a threshold and then apply the fixed threshold to the test set. For AUROC, the continuous PPL-derived score is used directly. The method is simple and model-internal, but it cannot distinguish factuality from surface naturalness.

\subsection{Hidden-State Probing}

The SAPLMA-style detector extracts a hidden representation from the frozen LLM and trains a classifier over it. For a statement \(x\), the model produces hidden states at each Transformer block. A layer \(l\) and pooling rule \(p\) define a feature vector:

\begin{equation}
h(x) = \mathrm{pool}_p(H_l(x)).
\end{equation}

We evaluate logistic regression and MLP classifiers. Features are standardized before classification. The classifier seeds are 42, 123, and 2024; reported SAPLMA values are test-set means and standard deviations across those seeds. The final-layer baseline uses layer 27 with last-token pooling. Later analysis varies the layer and pooling choice.

\subsection{Layer and Token-Pooling Analysis}

Layer analysis trains one logistic probe per Transformer block using last-token pooling. The validation split selects the operating layer, and the selected layer is then evaluated on test data. This avoids selecting the layer by test performance. Token-pooling analysis fixes the final layer and compares last-token, mean-token, and first-token representations.

\subsection{Attention-Guided Extensions}

The advanced method family tests whether attention contributes useful factuality information. The dataset consists of statements, so each statement is mapped to simple anchors: subject tokens, relation tokens, tail tokens, and the final valid token. These anchors approximate the entity, relation, and tail evidence that a statement-level detector should inspect. For each layer and head, we summarize attention from the last token to these anchors.

We evaluate several feature families. Raw attention-score features are computed for all candidate heads to test whether attention patterns carry a direct factuality signal. Debiased attention features remove length-related effects through residualization, because raw attention statistics can be confounded by sequence length and anchor count. Top-head features keep the heads with the strongest validation AUROC, testing whether validation-based head selection can reduce noisy dimensions. Attention-output features summarize selected attention output activations, capturing what the attention module writes back into the residual stream. Fusion methods concatenate these features with the hidden-state baseline or use a gated decision rule, testing whether hidden and attention signals are complementary at prediction time. The main variants are A0/A0s through A9, where A0/A0s are hidden-only baselines, A2 is the strongest full-data A0-A9 attention-guided variant, and A6/A8/A9 test top-head fusion, attention-output fusion, and gated routing.

This design keeps an exploration ladder from simple to complex uses of attention. A1/A2 test attention scores directly, A3/A6 test head selection, A4/A7 test attention-output activations, A5/A8 test feature concatenation, and A9 tests gated routing. This structure identifies the strongest A0-A9 attention-guided variant and the boundary cases where additional feature complexity does not improve full-data results.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why PPL is weak.}
PPL measures how well the model predicts a sequence. It is sensitive to sentence length, lexical frequency, common phrasing, and fluency. These factors correlate imperfectly with truth. The PPL distribution shows a higher false-statement tail, but the overlap is too large for a stable binary detector. This explains why PPL has moderate AUROC but poor accuracy and macro-F1.

\paragraph{Why hidden states work.}
Hidden states summarize more than final token probabilities. They encode semantic composition, entity-relation information, and model-internal consistency signals. The strong performance of logistic probes indicates that a substantial part of the factuality signal is linearly readable. The MLP improvement shows that non-linear boundaries can extract additional information, but the layer analysis shows that representation choice is at least as important as classifier complexity.

\paragraph{Why middle-to-late layers are strongest.}
The layer sweep suggests that factuality is not maximally available at the embedding-like bottom layer or necessarily at the final layer. Early layers mostly encode local lexical and syntactic information. Middle-to-late layers have integrated enough context to expose factuality signals. The final layer may be more specialized for immediate next-token prediction, which can make it less ideal as a general factuality representation.

\paragraph{Why debiased attention scores help.}
The attention features preserve structured alignment information between the final token and statement anchors. Hidden states compress the entire statement into a dense vector, whereas attention scores expose where the model routes information. Length residualization reduces sentence-length and anchor-count confounds, and the resulting A2 features outperform the hidden-only baseline on the full test set. The broader A0-A9 sweep shows that stable attention-score readout is more reliable here than top-head fusion, attention-output fusion, or the current gated routing rule.

\paragraph{Engineering lessons.}
The Phase 4 rerun also shows that numerical stability is part of the method. On the tested RTX 3090 environment, eager attention with float16 produced NaNs, while eager attention with bfloat16 was stable for hidden states, attention scores, and attention outputs. Reproducible probing therefore requires documenting dtype and attention implementation, not only model and dataset.

\section{Conclusion}

This project shows that LLM internal states provide substantially stronger hallucination-detection signals than output perplexity alone. On the full test set, SAPLMA-style hidden-state probes outperform PPL by large margins in accuracy, macro-F1, and AUROC. Layer and token analyses show that factuality signals are strongest in middle-to-late layers, and last-token pooling is much more reliable than first-token or mean pooling. Attention-guided features add a further interpretable extension: full Phase 4 ablation selects debiased attention-score-only features (A2) as the strongest attention-guided detector in the A0-A9 comparison, reaching 81.93\% accuracy, 81.93\% macro-F1, and 0.9010 AUROC. Future work should test this result on larger and different model families and design more robust calibration or routing mechanisms.

\section*{Limitations}

The experiments use a single base model, Qwen2-1.5B, and one processed True-False statement dataset. The task is binary and statement-level, so it is simpler than real user-facing hallucination detection in open-ended generation. Phase 4 is evaluated on the full split, but the A2 attention-guided conclusion still needs cross-model, cross-dataset, and significance validation. The anchor extraction rules are simple and may be less reliable for complex syntax. Finally, attention visualizations are diagnostic evidence only. They help explain model behavior and do not establish causal mechanisms.
